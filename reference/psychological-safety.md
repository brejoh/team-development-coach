# Psychological Safety

Use this when a supervisor senses something is wrong with their team but cannot articulate it, people are quiet, disengaged, or not raising issues. Psychological safety is often the invisible cause.

---

## What It Is

Psychological safety is the belief that you will not be punished or humiliated for speaking up, for sharing ideas, raising concerns, asking questions, or admitting mistakes.

It is not about being comfortable. It is about being willing to take interpersonal risks.

The concept comes from researcher Amy Edmondson, who found it to be the single biggest factor in team performance across Google's research into its own teams.

## Why It Breaks Down in Restructures

Restructures disrupt the conditions that psychological safety depends on:

- **New relationships**: people do not yet know if they can trust the new team or supervisor
- **Role uncertainty**: people protect themselves when they are unsure of their standing
- **Fear of judgement**: in a new structure, mistakes feel more visible and more costly
- **Loss of the old team**: people may be grieving a previous team where they did feel safe

## Signs It Is Low

- Meetings are quiet, the same one or two people speak
- No one raises problems until they are already serious
- People agree in the room and complain in the corridor
- Mistakes are hidden rather than discussed
- The supervisor is the last to know when something goes wrong


## What a Supervisor Can Do

**Model vulnerability.** Admit when you do not know something. Acknowledge your own mistakes. This signals that it is safe to do the same.

**Reward speaking up.** When someone raises a concern or a difficult question, thank them explicitly. Make it visible that doing so is valued.

**Separate safety from accountability.** Psychological safety does not mean lowering standards. A team can be both high-safety and high-accountability. The supervisor holds both.

**Be consistent.** Safety is built slowly and destroyed quickly. One overreaction to bad news can undo weeks of trust-building.
