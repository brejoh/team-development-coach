# Facilitation Techniques

Use these when a supervisor needs to engage their team in a structured conversation but isn't sure how. Each technique is designed to create participation without requiring the supervisor to have all the answers.

## 1. Check-In Round

**What it is:** At the start of a meeting, go around the room and ask everyone to answer one question in one or two sentences. No discussion until everyone has spoken.

**When to use it:** When you want to hear from everyone, not just the loudest voices. Good for building psychological safety in early-stage teams.

**Example prompt:** "In one sentence, how are you showing up today?"

## 2. Start, Stop, Continue

**What it is:** Ask the team to identify: one thing they should start doing, one thing they should stop, and one thing that is working and should continue.

**When to use it:** When a team needs to reflect on how they are working together. Good for teams in Norming who are ready to self-assess.

**How to run it:** Give people five minutes to write their answers individually, then share and discuss. The supervisor goes last.

## 3. 1-2-4-All

**What it is:** A structured discussion method. People reflect individually (1), then discuss in pairs (2), then in groups of four (4), then share with the whole team (All).

**When to use it:** When you want depth of thinking before group discussion. Prevents the first voice in the room dominating the conversation.

**When to use it in a restructure:** Ideal for sensitive topics like ways of working or team norms, where people need time to think before they speak.

## 4. Appreciative Inquiry

**What it is:** Instead of asking "what's not working," ask "when have we been at our best?" Then build from that.

**When to use it:** When a team is stuck in a negative narrative, particularly common post-restructure. Shifts energy without dismissing real concerns.

**Example prompt:** "Tell me about a time this team did something you were proud of. What made that possible?"

## 5. Working Agreements

**What it is:** A short, team-generated list of agreements about how the group will work together. Typically five to eight items. Posted visibly and revisited regularly.

**When to use it:** Early in a new team's life, or when norms need to be reset. Gives the team ownership of its own culture.

**Example agreements:** "We start on time." "We raise issues directly, not in the corridor." "We assume good intent."
