# Team Charter Template

A team charter is a living document that a team builds together. It is not something the supervisor writes and hands down, the value is in the conversation that creates it. Use this template when a supervisor wants to establish clarity and shared ownership with their team.

## Sections

### 1. Purpose
*Why does this team exist? What would be missing if we weren't here? How does our work connect to what the organisation is trying to achieve?*

**Rationale:** Post-restructure teams often lose their sense of purpose. Reestablishing it gives people a reason to invest in the new team. Connecting that purpose to organisational objectives makes it real, not just motivational language.

**Facilitator prompt:** "If we disappeared tomorrow, what would the organisation lose?"

**Follow-up prompt:** "How does what we do connect to what the organisation is trying to achieve this year?"

### 2. Values and Ways of Working
*How do we treat each other? What does each value look like in action, and what does it look like when we're not living it?*

**Rationale:** Values without behaviours are just words on a wall. Above and below the line thinking makes values visible and specific, so they can be celebrated and challenged.

**Above the line** behaviours are what the value looks like when the team is living it well.
**Below the line** behaviours are what slipping on that value looks like in practice.

| Value | Above the line | Below the line |
|-------|---------------|----------------|
| Respect | Listening fully before responding | Talking over people in meetings |
| Accountability | Flagging a problem early | Staying quiet and hoping it goes away |
| Collaboration | Sharing information proactively | Hoarding knowledge or working in silos |

**Facilitator prompt:** "Think of the best team you've been on. What made it work?"

**Follow-up prompt:** "What does that value look like on a good day, and what does it look like when we're not living up to it?"

### 3. Roles and Responsibilities
*Who owns what? Where do our responsibilities begin and end?*

**Rationale:** Restructures leave role clarity in a mess. This section surfaces overlap and gaps before they become conflict.

**Facilitator prompt:** "Where do you feel unclear about who owns what?"

### 4. Decision Making
*Who decides what? When do we decide as a team versus the supervisor deciding alone?*

**Rationale:** Unclear decision-making is one of the most common sources of team frustration. Being explicit prevents resentment.

**Facilitator prompt:** "Give me an example of a decision we made recently. Who should have owned that?"

### 5. Communication
*How do we keep each other informed? What goes in a meeting versus a message?*

**Rationale:** Communication norms set expectations and reduce unnecessary noise.

**Facilitator prompt:** "When do you feel most out of the loop?"

### 6. Handling Conflict
*What do we do when we disagree? How do we raise issues with each other?*

**Rationale:** Teams that name how they will handle conflict are far more likely to handle it well when it arrives.

**Facilitator prompt:** "If you had a problem with how I was working, how would you want to raise it?"

### 7. Operating Cadence
*When do we meet, why, and what for? How do we stay connected without drowning in meetings?*

**Rationale:** Post-restructure teams often inherit a meeting schedule nobody designed. Naming the cadence intentionally gives the team a rhythm and signals that the supervisor respects people's time.

**Types of meetings to define:**

| Meeting type | Purpose | Suggested frequency |
|---|---|---|
| Team meeting | Alignment, updates, shared decisions | Weekly or fortnightly |
| 1:1 | Individual check-in, development, feedback | Fortnightly |
| Retrospective | How are we working together? What needs to change? | Monthly |
| Ad hoc | Issues that can't wait | As needed, not as default |

**Facilitator prompt:** "Which of our current meetings actually need to exist? What would we cancel if we could?"

**Follow-up prompt:** "What meeting do we not have that we probably should?"

---

## How to Use This

A charter does not need to cover every section in one session. Start with Purpose and Ways of Working. Return to the others over time. The conversation matters more than the document.
